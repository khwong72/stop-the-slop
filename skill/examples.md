# Before/After Examples

Clean transformations organized by pattern. Use for reference when auditing.

---

## False Agency

**Before:**
> The complaint becomes a fix. The feedback loop tightens. The product improves.

**After:**
> A user complained, so the team shipped a fix that week. They built a tighter feedback loop. The product got better because people acted fast.

**What changed:** Named the actors (user, team, people). Objects don't do things — humans do.

---

## Narrator Voice

**Before:**
> Nobody designed this system. It emerged over time. People tend to underestimate how these patterns form organically.

**After:**
> You didn't sit down and design this. It built up over years of small decisions. You probably underestimate how much of your system is accidental.

**What changed:** "Nobody" → "You." Put the reader in the room instead of observing from above.

---

## Wh- Openers

**Before:**
> What makes this hard is the complexity of the system. Why this matters is that most teams don't have time to untangle it.

**After:**
> The system is complex. Most teams don't have time to untangle it.

**What changed:** Removed the Wh- scaffolding. The point lands faster without the setup.

---

## Vague Declaratives

**Before:**
> The implications are significant. The stakes are high. The reasons are structural.

**After:**
> This means your CAC doubles. You lose the deal if you're slow. The architecture won't support it without a rewrite.

**What changed:** Named the specific implications, stakes, and reasons. Vague → concrete.

---

## Negative Runway

**Before:**
> It wasn't the technology. It wasn't the team. It wasn't the market timing. It was the distribution strategy.

**After:**
> It was the distribution strategy.

**What changed:** Cut the dramatic buildup. The reader doesn't need the runway.

---

## Three-Item Lists

**Before:**
> Align. Execute. Iterate. That's the playbook every startup needs to follow.

**After:**
> Execute and iterate. Alignment happens along the way.

**What changed:** Three items → two. Cut the "playbook" framing. Reads less formulaic.

---

## Question Endings

**Before:**
> The best teams optimize for learning, not productivity. Are you doing this? What would you change?

**After:**
> The best teams optimize for learning, not productivity. Start there.

**What changed:** Removed the CTA questions. Ended on action.

---

## Over-Explanation

**Before:**
> The OODA loop (which stands for Observe, Orient, Decide, Act) was developed by military strategist John Boyd to describe how fighter pilots win dogfights. This framework, which has been adopted by business strategists, helps us understand decision-making speed.

**After:**
> The OODA loop came from fighter pilots. The pilot who cycles faster wins. Same applies to startups.

**What changed:** Trusted the reader to know or look up OODA. Cut the academic setup.

---

## Adverb Infestation

**Before:**
> This is genuinely important. It's fundamentally about trust. Teams that truly understand this inevitably outperform.

**After:**
> This matters. It's about trust. Teams that understand this outperform.

**What changed:** Killed genuinely, fundamentally, truly, inevitably. The sentences are stronger without them.

---

## Passive Hiding

**Before:**
> Mistakes were made. The decision was reached after much deliberation. It was believed that the strategy would work.

**After:**
> We made mistakes. The team decided after a week of debate. They believed the strategy would work.

**What changed:** Named who made mistakes, who decided, who believed. Passive → active.

---

## Compound Example: Full Transformation

**Before:**
> Here's the kicker: hiring senior engineers is broken. Not because the talent isn't there. Because the process filters for the wrong signals. What makes this frustrating is that everyone knows interviews don't work. The implications are massive. Let that sink in.
>
> Are you rethinking your hiring process yet?

**After:**
> Hiring senior engineers is broken because the process filters for the wrong signals — whiteboard puzzles instead of system design conversations. Everyone knows this. Few companies fix it because rewriting the loop takes six months.

**What changed:**
- Removed "Here's the kicker" throat-clearing
- Removed "Not X. Because Y." binary contrast
- Removed "What makes this frustrating" Wh- opener
- Removed "everyone knows" narrator voice → kept but added specifics
- Removed "implications are massive" vague declarative → named the real blocker (six months)
- Removed "Let that sink in" emphasis crutch
- Removed question ending
- Result: 3 sentences instead of 7. Added concrete detail (whiteboard puzzles, six months).
